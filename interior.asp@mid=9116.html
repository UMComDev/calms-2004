Tony winner gets cheers from her United Methodist congregation

June 21, 2005

By Linda Bloom*

NEW YORK (UMNS) — When Victoria Clark won the 2005 Tony Award for best actress in a musical, it was recognition for what has been the role of her career.

As Margaret Johnson in the new musical, "The Light in the Piazza," by Adam Guettel and Craig Lucas, Clark has impressed critics and theater-goers alike with her emotional depth and extraordinary voice.

But at the Church of St. Paul and St. Andrew, United Methodist, on the Upper West Side of Manhattan, she also has won raves for directing the children’s Christmas pageant.

The congregation has cheered her on as "The Light in the Piazza" progressed from runs in Seattle and Chicago to Broadway.

"People knew that she’d been working on this role as kind of a life’s work," said the Rev. James "K" Karpen, the church’s pastor.

Other Tony winners for "Piazza" were Guettel, best original score and, with Ted Sperling and Bruce Coughlin, best orchestrations; Michael Yeargan, best scenic design; Catherine Zuber, best costume design; and Christopher Akerlind, best lighting design.

In addition to the Tony, Clark was named best actress in a musical by the Drama Desk and Outer Critics Circle. She also received a Joseph Jefferson Award in 2004 in Chicago during "Piazza’s" run there.

Given the acclaim from critics, the awards were not a surprise. In his review of "The Light in the Piazza" for the New York Times, Ben Brantley wrote that Clark’s portrayal of Margaret Johnson "is hands down the best musical performance by an actress this season." The show’s run at Lincoln Center has been extended to Jan. 1.

Set in Italy in 1953, "The Light in the Piazza" follows Johnson, the wife of a successful American businessman, who is touring the country with her daughter, Clara. When a romance blossoms between Clara and Fabrizio, a handsome young Florentine, she tries to keep them apart as well as keep a secret about Clara. She is forced to reconsider both Clara’s future and her own hopes.

What occurs over the course of the musical is a personal, sexual and even spiritual awakening for her character, according to Clark. "I think Margaret has denied the spiritual side of herself," she added.

When her 10-year-old son, Thomas Luke, walked her down the red carpet before the Tony awards show began, a television interviewer asked him if his mom was going to win. When he said yes, the interviewer asked him why. "She has worked so hard, she really deserves it," he replied.

That hard work has encompassed more than 20 years of stage experience in New York, as well as studies and performances in opera, forays into directing and the teaching of vocal techniques to others.

Her own vocal training began at a young age. "Church was always the main venue for singing," she recalled about her childhood in Dallas, where her family attended a Congregational church. "It still is one of my favorite places to sing. It’s like saying ‘thank you’ for everything."

After graduating from Yale University with a degree in music, Clark moved to New York. She found St. Paul and St. Andrew in 1983, "started singing in the choir the second Sunday" and joined shortly thereafter. Since then, she has been active in both music and children’s ministry, including a five-year run as a Sunday school teacher.

Clark’s big passion, according to Karpen, has been the Christmas pageant, which she has directed several times. She helps the kids figure out their parts and treats them like professional actors, he said.

"I think Vicki believes that everybody’s got gifts and talents, and her role on this planet is helping people figure them out," he explained.

Her Broadway debut came in 1985 with "Sunday in the Park with George." She started attracting notice for her vocals in "A Grand Night for Singing," a tribute to Rodgers and Hammerstein, in 1993-94, and for her performance as Smitty in the 1995-96 revival of "How to Succeed in Business Without Really Trying," which also featured Matthew Broderick. Featured roles in "Titanic," "Cabaret" and "Urinetown" followed.

Her connection with Guettel began in 1994, when Clark was preparing one of the two fund-raising concerts she has performed for the church’s interfaith program. Guettel was a friend of Sperling, who knew Clark from Yale, and Clark asked if she could use one of his songs in the concert.

Clark admired "Floyd Collins," another musical by Guettel, as well as the work of Lucas, who wrote the book for "The Light in the Piazza." Sperling, who is in charge of music direction for "Piazza," had played one of the songs from the show, "Fable," for her a couple of years earlier. "When I read the script and the novella by Elizabeth Spencer, I knew it was up my alley," she said.

Vocally, the role of Margaret Johnson is demanding. Clark credits her voice teacher of 18 years, Edward Sayegh, for keeping her soprano in shape even during periods when her roles did not require a broad vocal range.

While playing Margaret is emotionally exhausting, Clark said the role is her most satisfying part to date. The supporting roles of the past never allowed for the depth of expression that she has found in Margaret Johnson. "It was never really enough stage time for me to say what I wanted to say," she explained.

Clark considers Guettel "deeply, deeply spiritual," and she finds a lot of Christology in "The Light from the Piazza." The light can mean many things, including compassion, redemption and grace. "For me, the light is about the fact that everyone, including God, has forgiven Margaret for this burden she is carrying," she said.

She offers her own prayers to God before and after each performance and communes with a statue of the Holy Mother, built into the set, during a brief wait in the wings. "We have a chat every night."

Clark appreciates the support of the St. Paul and St. Andrew congregation but admitted she is shy about promoting her accomplishments, being mindful of the "many talented people" in the congregation.

Her best present when she returned home after the Tony awards show, she said, was the homemade cards waiting from Karpen’s children and another church member.

May and June were hectic—Clark continued giving eight performances a week while recording the cast album, doing award-related publicity sessions, participating in Tony show rehearsals and attending award presentations—but she is grateful for the recognition.

"It’s the crazy you dream about, it’s the fatigue you want," she said.
