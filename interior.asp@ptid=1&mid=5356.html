Volunteers Press On, Despite International Unrest. But Not All Work Abroad.

By Lesley Crosson

(UMCom) -- When Sandy Rowland of Charles Town, W. Va., voiced her wish to return to Bosnia for a third mission trip "nobody wanted me to go," she says.

It was May 2003, two months after the start of the U.S.-led war on Iraq. The memory of the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks on New York City and Washington, D.C., still was fresh. And, to make matters worse, this time she planned to travel by herself.

"I was going to cancel if the government or the church said it was dangerous to go," she says. But the threat posed by wartime travel was not the only factor in her decision-making. "I prayed about it, and I said Iâ€™m going, war or no war."

Rowland, in her early 50s, lost her husband and father-in-law in a 1997 car accident. Her previous trips to Bosnia had been with mission teams. Now she wanted to return to help village women share their grief over lost husbands and support their healing.

"The Lord called me to go back as an individual volunteer," she says. "I had met so many women who were widows, and since Iâ€™m a widow myself I felt a strong urge to share with them how to go through their grief."

During the three-month trip she lived with two Muslim families, one of them headed by a widow. Rowland was told the widow "blossomed" after sharing her home.

"Itâ€™s because we had something in common," Rowland remembers. "We were able to talk to each other through her children, who spoke English. Sometimes all it takes is for somebody who cares."

Rowlandâ€™s attitude is shared by many United Methodists who, even in a climate of international danger and fear, continue to answer the call to mission, at home and abroad.

Jeanie Blankenbaker, mission volunteers executive for the General Board of Global Ministries, says there was "some drop-off" in mission trips immediately after the Sept. 11 attacks, but that participation started climbing steadily in January 2002 and now is "stronger than ever."

In June alone, Blankenbaker reports, the board processed more than $12,000 in insurance applications for 2,484 volunteers departing for international mission trips.

Rowland, who attends Leetown United Methodist Church in Charlestown and serves as volunteer mission coordinator for the Baltimore-Washington annual conference, emphasizes that people can and do help without leaving their communities.

Just last month, she returned from a month-long trip to Nigeria, where her team worked with United Methodist volunteers in Iowa to build a science lab and refurbish a school in the town of Jalingo. This is not the only way in which Rowland has involved people from home. In 1989, Rowland's team visited a poor Nigerian village where children were dressed in rags.  Last year she thought of sending dresses to the children and enlisted volunteers to make them.

The team delivered the dresses last month. Rowland says she is waiting to see pictures of the kids "so that all the people here who made the dresses can see that their time, energy and money were well spent. Each one of them helped to keep a little child warm. I want that to be an ongoing process."

Rowland plans to go back to Nigeria next year, and she dreams of someday returning to Bosnia. But first, she will resume her work locally, assisting teams that are rebuilding thousands of homes destroyed when Hurricane Isabel ripped through several East Coast states last September.
