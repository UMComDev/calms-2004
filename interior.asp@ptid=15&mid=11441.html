CEF Board President’s Response to Recent Call for More and Better Lay Education

 A CEF Commentary by Linda Vogel
Linda Vogel

I was very interested in the Rev. Clayton Childers‘ thoughtful United Methodist News Service commentary about the many United Methodist Church members who are not aware of our Social Principles—found in the Discipline and central to our faith as United Methodist’s. A number of years ago, the General Board of Church and Society produced a fine video entitled The Retreat, which I have used with confirmation students as well as with high school students and adults. If you haven’t seen it, it is worth looking up.

When Dwight and I were serving at St. Luke’s UMC in Dubuque, Iowa, we told those in our membership classes that our Social Principles have always been an  evolving document which the General Conference amends from time to time as new understandings and prophetic voices keep the church vital and relevant in our changing world. But, we said, “If you agree with less than half of what is included in the Social Principles, this is probably not the denomination for you. If you agree with 85%, this is probably a good fit. We invite you to join with us in working for change to those areas where we may need to grow.”

Childers is absolutely right. Lay education is crucial to keep our denomination growing and vital. As he points out, more and more folks choose a congregation rather than a denomination (denominational loyalty has been declining for a number of years). Many in our congregations do not have any understanding of Wesleyan theology or of the UM ethos. We are NOT a doctrinal church and persons are not asked to affirm a particular UM statement of beliefs. We hold that Scripture, reason, tradition and (religious) experience offer a matrix out of which United Methodist’s confer with one another, pray, study and live out their discipleship. While “tradition” for Roman Catholics comes in the form of encyclicals and councils, UM tradition is embodied in Wesleyan hymns and sermons as well as in the Discipline.

Membership classes for all who choose to join our congregations are crucial. I have seen many persons leave our church when conflict arises because they have no understanding of how our itinerant system of appointing clergy works. No one who is a leader in a UM congregation should be shocked to learn that the congregation cannot “fire” their pastor.

In addition to studying the social principles and engaging in Bible study and discipleship education, United Methodists need to explore the benefits of small (covenant) groups. We do not grow in our faith by simply attending worship, though that is an integral part of church membership.

Persons of all ages need to be engaged in rigorous study that takes seriously the Bible and the context of their own community and of the world today. Congregations that are not wrestling with and responding to issues of peace with justice, world hunger, religious bigotry and intolerance, the sustainability of life on this planet, and the growing chasm between the rich and the poor in the United States and around the world are falling short of God’s claim on our lives to be a light to the nations and the body of Christ for the world.

CEF and church educators (both clergy and lay) are called to create safe and hospitable spaces where Christians dare to wrestle with the hard questions of death and life, of justice and peace, of poverty and affluence. If we are not challenging persons to think differently and to act counter-culturally, we are not living fully into our calling.
