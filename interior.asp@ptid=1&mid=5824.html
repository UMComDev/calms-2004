Cross-promoting Works for Oprah. It Can Work at Church, Too.

By Katherine Bontrager

(UMCom) -- The survival of any product or public personality depends on on-going promotion. Oprah Winfrey doesn’t let you forget her name or face – you see it in magazines, newspapers and on television. And she is consistently cross-promoting her various outlets – her magazine publicizes her Web site, which heralds her TV show.

Churches have much to learn from such a marketing maven. To garner traffic in the pews and online, some cross-promotion may just be in order. And as with Winfrey and her shiny new Pontiacs, sometimes the most unique marketing stunts are the most effective.

John Halsey offered just such a marketing drive a year and a half ago to his 132-year-old First United Methodist Church in Birmingham, Ala. At the time, the church’s promotions were antiquated, and the congregation wasn’t reaching its growing community.

Halsey focused on overhauling the church’s promotions and launching a new Web site. “I’m a big fan of branding, and I wanted to use marketing to reach people and show them all the church has to offer,” he says. 

The result was a dynamic Web site offering not only the typical calendar of events but a breakdown of the church’s beliefs, a call to ministries and countless other outlets for readers. But the next challenge was to drive traffic to the site, not only from the congregation, but also from the larger community.

Halsey caught everyone’s attention with the catchy slogan, Faith Happens. The motto translated into a hard-to-forget Web address, www.faithhappens.org.

“We worked to cross-pollinate using several different vehicles,” he says. Radio spots on contemporary rock stations and bumper stickers improved the site’s visibility.

But Halsey didn’t stop with ordinary venues. The church got creative and rolled out movie screen stills at local theatres to advertise the church’s growing Web site. And to mark Birmingham’s music festival, the church acquired hundreds of water bottles and branded them with the URL and the phrase, “What You’re Thirsty For.” The promotion was a success.

“We also did what we called guerilla promotions,” he says. “We had banners made up that we hung over the interstate or other busy intersections that promoted our Web site. We had 10,000-some clicks in a very short period. It drove great traffic to the Web site.”

The church found three new members in the first six months off the site. A year later, they had 30 to 40 new members. The promotion cost about $8,000. “Talk about a return on your investment. The new members totally justified the funds spent,” he says.

Catching the community’s eye and earning a return on an investment is something the group Servant Evangelism specializes in.

“We started going into the community of Cincinnati some 20 years ago,” says Steve Sjogren, who pioneered the idea of Servant Evangelism and now provides conferences on the topic. “Our Vineyard Community Church startup wasn’t going well. After talking to nearly 2,000 people in the first two years that we were in town, we had just 37 people show up on our first week of services. That was incredibly disappointing.”

Sjogren found divine inspiration to focus outward rather than inward. “The first project was a free carwash,” he says. “We set up signs that read, ‘Totally Free Car Wash’ and ‘Free Car Wash – No Kidding.’ People in Cincinnati are pretty skeptical! We had to break through that layer of skepticism to get to their hearts.”

After months of diligently going out several times a week, the church began to get the city’s attention. “We began to branch out and do other projects like windshield washing, water bottle giveaways and free gift wrapping at the mall at Christmas.”

Their efforts blossomed, and their Web site, www.servantevangelism.com, now boasts 300 projects, including distributing coins for parking meters, batteries for smoke detectors and free donuts during morning traffic, all projects steeped in evangelism. Roughly 1000 people visit the site a day, most looking for new project ideas for their own churches. Always available through his e-mail address, stevesjogren@mac.com, Sjogren offers congregations valuable advice for starting their own Servant Evangelism. Among those who chose to rely on these random acts of kindness is the Journey Church in New York City.

“The Journey Church in Manhattan is three years old and has grown to 750 solely through doing Servant Evangelism according to them,” he says. “They serve far more than come to their church certainly, but they will grow very large in the coming years.” 

Unique promotions, whether they use water bottles, banners, coins, or donuts, publicize to the community not only the name of the church but its underlying values of faith and support. Such a solid base of commitment would make even Winfrey proud. 

Katherine Bontrager is freelance writer based in Chicago where she lives with her husband and their assorted four-legged friends.
