Living Into The Future
A Report of the General Council on Ministries
To the 2004 General Conference of The United Methodist Church
Background
In his book, An Unfinished Church, ...a Brief History of the Union..., the late Bishop Paul
Washburn clearly articulates the hopes of the new United Methodist Church. He points
out that the Joint Commission on Union acknowledged that it did not accommodate the
“clamoring in the church for a more drastic revision of the church’s boards and
agencies,” settling instead for matching up parallel agencies of the predecessor
denominations and seeking to identify competent leadership.
In reporting to the 1972 General Conference, the Structure Study Commission’s chair,
Dr. Dow Kirkpatrick, observed, “what we have heard in these four years from the
church at all levels clearly is that the elements in the board and agency structures of The
United Methodist Church which offer the least power for mission in the future ...are
those elements which cannot be dealt with by amending one board here and another one
there.” “Rather,” he goes on, “these elements are part of the whole system and they
must be dealt with in their wholeness if this system is to be renewed.”
The United Methodist Church, created in 1968, was born with a felt need to study its
general church structures and improve them. The Structure Study Commission was the
first effort.
In the early 1980’s the church commissioned a series of seventeen studies presented in
books entitled Into Our Third Century. These studies, initiated by the General Council on
Ministries and encouraged by the Council of Bishops, produced an extensive analysis of
selected issues of fundamental ministry and organizational concerns to the
denomination and produced a study of the environment in which United Methodism in
the United States serves.
During the 1989-92 quadrennium, the General Council on Ministries became aware of
increasing interest in and concern about structures and their effectiveness in enabling
The United Methodist Church to fulfill its mission. As a result of the Council’s work
during the 1989-92 quadrennium, the 1992 General Conference mandated a
Connectional Issues Study to “lead the church in a time of discernment and study of its
mission and structural needs...” and recommend back to the next General Conference.
The Connectional Issues Study Task Force recommended “alternative organizational
strategies for maximum missional effectiveness.”
In receiving the Connectional Issues Study report, the 1996 General Conference renewed
the call for analysis and created the Connectional Process Team to lead the church in
discerning what structural changes might enhance United Methodist efforts to be
faithfully in mission for Christ in service to the world.
Mandate
Meeting in Cleveland, Ohio, on May 12, 2000, the General Conference of The United
Methodist Church voted down the recommendations of the Connectional Process Team
and mandated that the General Council on Ministries “determine the most effective
design for the work of the general agencies and...provide enabling legislation to the
2004 General Conference.
This mandate narrowed the focus of the continuous discussion of the last three decades
to one specific question-What is the most effective design for the work of the general
agencies of The United Methodist Church? The General Council on Ministries has
worked diligently to discern an answer to that question and is pleased to present this
report entitled Living Into The Future.
Process
The members and staff of the General Council on Ministries have listened intently and
carefully to members of the church across the United States and in the central
conferences. Consultations have been held with representatives of the general agencies,
annual conferences, ethnic groups and caucuses, seminaries, and others affiliated with
The United Methodist Church. For three years, the Council has sought prayerfully to
discern what is the most effective design for the relationship among these important
agents of ministry.
In 2001 the Servant Leadership Team (executive committee of the General Council on
Ministries) commissioned a writing team with the charge to bring forward the best
synthesis of what was gleaned from the listening effort and what was judged to be the
best proposal for consideration by the Council. The proposal brought forward failed to
gain the members’ support, although elements of it were endorsed and forwarded to a
new writing team which, in 2001, was commissioned by the Council to further study,
consult and discern what best would meet the expectations of the General Conference
and the needs of the church for coordinated, collaborative ministry carried out by the
general agencies.
In a series of five meetings, electronic and face-to-face, the General Council on Ministries
has arrived at a clear sense that what is presented herein meets those criteria. The
values and assumptions which underlay and supported the work of the Council in
responding to the mandate of the General Conference are outlined in the narrative
portion of the proposal. The proposal represents a bold, first step to create an
environment for ongoing discernment in faithfulness to the objective that the church
might be more holistic, more collaborative, more efficient and more agile in its work at
the general agency level.
The General Council on Ministries completed work on the document in September 2003
and prayerfully offers this proposal as a faithful response to its mandate.
LIVING INTO THE FUTURE PROPOSAL
Historical Grounding
From the beginning of the organization of the church at the Christmas Conference of
1784, our church has engaged in collaborative and conciliar decision making. At that
conference the members, both pastors and lay preachers, determined together their
salaries and outlined the work for the coming year. When plans were made to ordain
Francis Asbury at the request of John Wesley, he was willing to accept ordination to
Superintendent (Bishop) "only if they elect me." From that day to this "As United
Methodists, we respond to the working (of the Holy Spirit) through a connectional
polity based upon mutual responsiveness and accountability." (Book of Discipline, 2000,
�101.1) From that day to this we have been guided both by elected leaders and
conciliar bodies acting on behalf of the whole .
General Conference Mandate
The General Conference, the central conciliar body of The United Methodist Church,
received in the year 2000 the report of the Connectional Process Team, amended the
report and adopted a motion that included the following mandate to the General
Council on Ministries:
3. To determine the most effective design for the work of the general agencies
and to provide implementing legislation to the 2004 General Conference.
The GCOM has responded to that mandate and now submits proposed legislation in
the materials that follow this preamble. We offer this work to the General Conference as
we proceed together to determine the will of God for our general church structures in
the immediate future. Although the General Conference directed the GCOM to speak
only to the most effective design for the work of the general agencies, it is envisioned
that a close relationship with the Council of Bishops is integral to an effective design.
In keeping with its constitutional mandate "to initiate and direct all connectional
enterprises of the Church...” (�15.8 ) the General Conference has, from time to time,
altered the relationship among the parts. However, it has consistently perpetuated both
the conciliar nature and the connectional structure of the whole. The work of the
general agencies is complex, yet there is a remarkable connection among them, a
connection to each other, the annual conferences and the local churches. Collaboration,
presently strong and growing can be enhanced as we work together seamlessly,
honoring our vital functions in service to Jesus Christ. It is essential that the general
agencies continue to grow as strategic partners in the shared ministry of the church .
Connectional Table
We believe that two additional issues need addressing to further strengthen the
connection. 1) While respecting the individual responsibilities of each of the general
agencies, a forum where all agencies and non-agency members have the opportunity
to give voice and vote on matters of common interest is needed. 2) The historic and
ongoing commitment of the UMC to financial integrity and independence has
inadvertently led to structures that separate ministry and financial decisions. There is a
need for a place where continuing discernment, articulation, and implementation of
God’s vision for the church can be undertaken in response to General Conference
legislation, Council of Bishops’ expressions and the needs of the world.
Therefore, we propose the creation of a Connectional Table as the most effective means
of addressing these matters. The purpose of such a body would be for the discernment and
articulation of a vision for the church and the stewardship of the mission, ministries and
resources of The United Methodist Church as determined by the actions of General Conference
and in consultation with the Council of Bishops.
This Connectional Table would need to integrate and synthesize the essential, current
work of the General Council on Ministries and the General Council on Finance and
Administration, keeping intact the work of all the other general agencies as currently
described in the Book of Discipline. It would engage in mutual discernment of God’s
vision for the church, and maintain accountability for our common task.
Keeping in mind that checks and balances, such as auditing functions, are essential to
responsible stewardship, such work must be continued. However, entrusting allocation
decisions to an elected table representing the interdependent network of members
familiar with the work of the agencies would foster sound decision making, reduce
duplication of effort and strengthen the connection.
Our church flourished and grew when it had strong leadership focused on articulating
core values, discerning and articulating a clear vision, and championing a flexible
system to deliver mission. A Connectional Table that is motivated by faithfulness to the
mission of the church to make disciples of Jesus Christ would work in the following
ways: It would be global in scope and holistic in understanding. It would be inclusive
in nature and collaborative in style. While being efficient in the stewardship of
resources, it would be transparent, accessible, and accountable in relationships.
About the Term “Council”
The constitution of The United Methodist Church provides for only two councils: the
Council of Bishops and the Judicial Council. We suggest that the designation of
"council" be reserved for only the bodies of the general church which have constitutional
bases.
Underlying Principles/Assumptions/Values
  • The annual conference is the basic unit of The UMC.
  • The UMC is uniquely conciliar and connectional.
  • The units of the church-local congregations, annual conferences, jurisdictional
    conferences, central conferences and general agencies-are essentially and
    fundamentally interdependent.
  • The UMC is called to inclusiveness and seeks to reflect in its structures the diversity
    of the church membership, denying every semblance of discrimination.
  • The UMC, in Africa, Europe, Asia and the United States, seeks to strengthen its
    understanding and expression of its global nature. The UMC seeks to strengthen its
    partnership in mission with Latin America and the Caribbean and other regions
    where autonomous affiliated Methodist Churches are present.
  • There is a need in The UMC for a place where the mission, needs and resources of
    the church may be seen from a holistic perspective.
  • Optimum stewardship of finances and ministries is possible when decisions are
    made at a “common table.”
  • The church’s effectiveness can be enhanced by the increased transparency and
    accessibility of its institutions.
  • The ministries of the general church can be enhanced by increased collaboration
    around overarching issues of mission while respecting the discreet responsibilities
    and portfolios of ministry assigned to each.
  • The general church can find in the local church and annual conference settings
    excellent examples of common table spirit and structure.
  • There may be value in general church structures which are replicable at the annual
    conference and local church level.
  • There is a need for a place where continuing discernment, articulation and
    implementation of God’s vision for the church can be undertaken in response to
    General Conference legislation, Council of Bishops’ expressions and the needs of the
    world.
  • There is an expressed need for efficiency and the elimination of duplication in the
    work of the institutions of the general church.
    Essential Functions of the Connectional Table (CT)
    Steward of the vision & resources for mission and ministry:
    1. To provide a forum for the understanding and implementation of the vision,
    mission and ministries of the global church as determined in consultation with
    the Council of Bishops and/or the action of the General Conference.
    2. To enable the flow of information and communication between and among
    annual conferences, jurisdictions, central conferences, general agencies, and the
    Council of Bishops.
    3. Consistent with actions of the General Conference, to align the program life of
    the church with the mandates of the gospel and the needs of the global
    community by listening to the expression of needs, addressing emerging issues,
    and determining the most effective, cooperative, and efficient way to provide
    optimum stewardship of ministries, personnel, and finances.
    4. To review and evaluate the effectiveness of the general agencies in fulfilling the
    ministries assigned to them.
    5. To evaluate the connectional structures of the church and recommend to the
    General Conference such legislative changes as may be appropriate
    to effect desirable modifications.
    6. To provide leadership in planning and research, assisting all levels of the church
    to evaluate needs and plan strategies to carry out the mission of the church.
    Fiscal accountability and management
    7. To be accountable to The United Methodist Church through the General
    Conference in all mattes relating to the receiving, disbursing, and reporting of
    funds authorized by the General Conference. In the exercise of its fiscal
    accountability role, the CT shall have the authority and responsibility to perform
    the following:
    a. To provide a finance committee to include persons with expertise
    (which shall include non-CT members) which shall be amenable to the
    CT whose decision shall be binding.
    b. To provide for an audit and review committee that shall
    include non-CT members with special expertise.
    c. To submit to each quadrennial session of the General Conference
    budgets of expense for each of the general funds of the church and to
    make recommendations regarding all other funding considerations to
    come before the General Conference.
    d. To receive and disburse general funds raised throughout the church in
    accordance with approved budgets.
    e. To require an annual audit of all treasuries receiving general church
    funds.
    f. To undertake an annual general agency budget review and approval.
    g. To ensure that all general funds expenditures are in compliance with
    the church’s social principles.
    h. To develop and implement such general investment policies as may be
    appropriate.
    i. To provide for investment oversight through such instruments
    as an investment committee which shall include non-CT
    members and persons with special expertise.
    j. To review and approve special offerings and church-wide appeals.
    k. To establish and implement policies governing the ownership, sale,
    rental and major renovation or purchase of properties by general
    agencies.
    Policies and Procedures
    8. To take all necessary legal steps to safeguard and protect the interests and rights of
    the denomination, which may include the provision of legal services to general
    agencies and the Council of Bishops.
    9. To provide a personnel policies committee to oversee such matters as may
    be appropriate, i.e. equitable and adequate compensation and benefits.
    10. To establish general policies to inform the personnel practices of the general
    agencies.
    11. To collect, organize and maintain such statistics as may be required to inform the
    planning and work of the church, i.e. the general minutes.
    12. To elect, upon nomination of the general agency boards, the general secretaries of
    the agencies.
    13. To provide for the staffing needs for the Connectional Table to assure fulfilling of the
    essential functions of the Table.
    Composition/Membership of the Connectional Table
    The voting members of the Connectional Table shall consist of a maximum of 131
    persons as follows:
  • 85 members elected through jurisdictional and central conferences (one from each of
    the 64 annual conferences in the United States, three from each of the seven central
    conferences) subject to the diversity objectives set forth below,* plus up to twelve
    members-at-large for balance of laity/clergy, racial/ethnic background, gender and
    geography. The members-at-large shall be nominated by a nominating process as
    determined by the Connectional Table.
  • Up to fourteen bishops to include:
  • the president of the Council of Bishops;
  • three bishops named at the discretion of the Council of Bishops to assure a
    minimum of three bishops from the central conferences;
  • plus bishops elected as presidents of agencies
  • general secretaries and the presidents, by virtue of office, of the following agencies:
    General Board of Church and Society, General Board of Discipleship, General Board
    of Global Ministries, General Board of Higher Education and Ministry, General
    Commission on Christian Unity and Interreligious Concerns, General Commission
    on Religion and Race, General Commission on Status and Role of Women, General
    Commission on United Methodist Men, General Commission on Communications,
    and General Commission on Archives and History.
    The general secretaries and presidents of the General Board of Pensions and Health
    Benefits and the United Methodist Publishing House may sit at the Table and shall have
    the right to the floor without privilege of voting.
    *Among elected members, it is recommended that the members of the Connectional
    Table be, insofar as possible, 50% clergy, 50% laity, 50% female, 50% male, not less than
    30% members of racial/ethnic minority groups, not less than 10% youth and young
    adults. Central conference members would not be counted among the racial/ethnic
    minority members.
    Two or more elected members of the Connectional Table may be deployed from the CT
    to each agency board to serve as additional members with vote effective January 2007.
    Between January 2005, and December 2006, the deployed members would be without
    vote at the general agency where sent.
