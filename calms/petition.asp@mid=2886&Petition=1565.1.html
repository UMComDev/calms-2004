Report No. 1
WORLD SERVICE FUND


  

Introduction
When United Methodist congregations pay their apportioned askings they participate in God’s work around the world...and right in their own parish. The World Service Fund is the heart of our church’s ministry together. Through this Fund you each of us becomespartner with the church’s agencies to be in mission and ministry at home and around the world. The effects of World Service ministry are making a difference across the globe.  
  
World Service is God’s people reaching out in love and compassion in the name of Christ. It represents a call and a challenge to each United Methodist. As The Book of Discipline 2000 states, the full payment of the World Service Fund is each congregation’s “first benevolent responsibility” (�812).
  
General Observations
This quadrennium has been a challenge for local churches, annual conferences and the general agencies as each dealt with the effects of September 11, wars, the world economy, the steep decline in the value of investments, instability within the U.S. stock market, spend down of reserves and double digit increases in health care costs. The local churches and annual conferences have struggled to meet their fixed and missional needs while remaining faithful to their connectional covenant. The general agencies have faced those same challenges and have made tough decisions adjusting their budgets in light of those realities.  
  
World Service Fixed Charges
General Council on Finance and Administration. The Council is accountable to The United Methodist Church through the General Conference for receiving, disbursing, and reporting all general funds. This line item is an estimate of the World Service Fund’s share of the Council’s expenses for the quadrennium. The Council also receives income from an allocation in the General Administration Fund, and earnings on invested funds.
  
Interpretation Resources. Along with producing materials to interpret United Methodism’s connectional funds, the Marketing Team of United Methodist Communications (UMCom) uses money from this section of the budget for “Promotion of Giving,” a joint effort of United Methodist Communications, the GCFA, the General Board of Discipleship (GBOD) and the General Board of Higher Education and Ministry (GBHEM). The “Promotion of Giving” goal is to develop comprehensive financial stewardship tools, leading to improved financial support of ministry at all levels of the connection. “Promotion of Giving” resources are financed with promotional money set aside from each of the denomination’s eight apportioned funds.

Program Agencies
The World Service Fund provides basic financial operating resources to four general program boards, four general commissions, and one organization. Allocations were developed with the General Council on Ministries (GCOM) following the procedures described in �806.1b), The Book of Discipline 2000.

The 2001-2004 World Service general program agency allocations were reduced based on the use of reserve funds, and included subsidies from the benefit trust as additional income streams. This action allowed the general church budget growth to be minimized while maximizing resources forcontinuing orministries. During the 2005-2008 quadrennium, reserves are not available for operating budgets and benefit trustis greatly reduced.these additional income streams will be available at greatly reduced amounts or not at all.
  
The $222 million proposal for the program agencies represents an actual operational increase of 2%. The losses of reserve funds and benefit trust, cited above, are also accounted for in the World Service proposal. When adopted, the World Service apportionment for the general agencies will have increased only 8.03% over four quadrennia - sixteen years.

During the budgeting process, presentations by the General Council on Ministries and the program agencies convinced the Council that 1) general church ministry would be severely eroded unless World Service funding for 2005-2008 increased substantially and 2) general agency executive staff have the vision, desire and skills required to reshape denominational ministry in exciting ways. The Council challenged the agencies to further develop their plans for efficiency and effectiveness in ministry. The agencies have proposed several actions in response to that challenge, including but not limited to the following:
  
  • Reductions in the number of meetings and size of some boards of directors.
    • Designating the General Board of Global Ministries to coordinate work in the central conferences while continuing to affirm the work of the General Board of Higher Education and Ministry with educational institutions outside the United States.
        
  • Work toward the deployment of collaborative and/or use designated local field staff in the geographic regions of the central conferences, as well as the possibility of shared staff in the United States.
    • Holding meetings in common venues to maximize cost savings and engender increased collaboration.

    • Creation of a new young peoples division within the General Board of Discipleship, effectively reducing the number of units by one.

    • Coordinate the scholarships and internships presently administered by several agencies.

    • Negotiate joint contracts for goods and services such as telephone, office equipment and supplies, etc., for agencies in Nashville, New York and Washington, D.C., to gain economies of scale.

    • Explore the contracting of a common travel agency for use by all of the general program agencies.
      
    Challenges and changes will continue during the 2005-2008 quadrennium as agencies address urgent priorities within the denomination such as clergy recruitment, deepening spirituality and the increasingly global nature of the church.

    We believe the agencies have made and continue to make visible improvements in organization and administration. We support the recommended World Service funding level for 2005-2008.
