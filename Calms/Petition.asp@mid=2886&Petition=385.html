Human Rights in Libya
        
Libya has been governed by its current dictator, Colonel Mu’ammar al-Qadhafi, since 1969. It has no formal constitution. The government controls society through local “revolutionary committees.” Political trials are held in secret. Arbitrary arrests and torture are common, according to international human rights groups. The death penalty is sometimes used for political opponents of the dictatorship. Dissidents overseas are sometimes abducted or murdered. There are no legal opposition parties. The government controls the media and most institutions of society.  Any public criticism of the government is illegal. “Purification Committees” monitor individuals and communications in search of possible dissent from government policies. The government controls most mosques and closely oversees most religious activity. Islamic law influences the government to suppress some liberties for women. Women cannot travel overseas without permission from their husbands. The government dominates the economy, and free labor unions and professional organizations are not permitted. There is no legal right for laborers to strike.    

THE ISSUES
  
Freedom of Speech - Free media does not exist in Libya. Anything that appears contrary to government is forbidden. As all media are controlled and run by the state, such thoughts that seem threatening are very rare. Western news channels, such as CNN, are available via satellite; however, foreign programming is often censored. The Internet is permitted; however, service is tolerated through only one provider, which is owned by Colonel Qadhafi’s son.
  
Free Elections - The Libyan people cannot change their government democratically. Colonel Qadhafi rules “by decree” with no accountability to any independent legislature or judiciary.  There are no free elections, and all major government positions are appointed by Qadhafi and his closest associates. The reigning ideology of Qadhafi is based on his “Green Book,” which combines Islam with socialism and nationalism.

Political Prisoners - Libya holds many political prisoners, however, this is commonly denied by authorities. It seems that political trials are often held in secret, with due process forbidden. Critics of political arrests are considered threatening and are themselves subject to arrest. Torture is common, and political prisoners are subject to the death penalty.  
  
Religious Freedom - The government publicly acknowledges its restriction on freedom of religion. Islamic clerics must support the government. Small Christian communities are permitted to worship openly. There is, however, a governmentally imposed restriction that there be no more than one Christian denomination per city. Church expansion is therefore difficult.    
  
OUR RESPONSE  

The United Methodist Church calls for protection of basic human rights and the creation of of democratic liberties in Libya. We pray for all Libyans, especially for the church in Libya. In particular, we pray for full religious freedom, a free press, free elections, equal rule of all for all people, and the abolition of torture. We shall be an advocate for human rights and democratic reforms within Libya in our interactions with the U.S. government and other governments, with ecumenical organizations, and directly with the Libyan government, when possible. We stand in solidarity with Libyan advocates of human rights, who are often in peril of their lives, whether in Libya or abroad.
Submitted ByMark Tooley
Alexandria VA
