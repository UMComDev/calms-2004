Rights of Persons. who have a Mental Illness and their families --- Both church and society have been slow to appreciate the heinous power stigma has over the lives of people who have mental illnesses and their families. We confess that we have not intentionally opened our hearts, doors and minds to the mentally ill, their loved ones and friends nor have we invited them to gather in God's loving, caring community. Instead, we have been silently complicit partners in the travesty of stigma-induced violation of the basic human rights of people who have mental illnesses.  People with psychiatric diagnoses and their families have the same needs for compassionate caring presence, love, and relationship as do we all. We therefore call upon churches, districts and annual conferences to intentionally welcome and integrate into the warp and woof of their worshipping and community lives those whom mental illness is most likely to impact: men, women, children; the young, the middle-aged the elderly; the poor, the middle-class, the rich; the married, the engaged, the single; the employed, the unemployed, the retired, the underemployed; people who have "physical" disabilities, people who have "mental" disabilities, people who have no disabilities. We also urge churches, districts and annual conferences to become intentional "Caring Communities" that seek to incarnate Christ's caring, loving presence to and with any who are mentally ill and their families. Knowing every one of us is created in God's own image, we joyfully affirm the sacred personhood of all persons whose lives are affected by mental illness. Finally, we profess the rights of the mentally ill to equal employment opportunity, affordable housing, access to health care, parity in medical insurance, and treatment that goes beyond group or supported/assisted living to integration/reintegration into our social communities and communities of faith.
