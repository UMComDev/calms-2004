Amend �163.E as follows:

E) Poverty-In spite of general affluence in the industrialized nations, the majority of persons in the world live  suffer the cruel deprivations of living in poverty. In order to provide basic needs such as food, clothing, shelter, education, health care, and other necessities, ways must be found to share more equitably the resources of the world.   In the United States a traditional "work ethic" requires that everyone carry his own weight, but some are unable to work because of age, illness, or care of other family members.  Numerous charities and government welfare programs must be continued to ameliorate the conditions of the poor, but they do not address the structures and functions that perpetuate poverty because these are deeply embedded in our culture.  The blight will continue until religious communities understand its causes.  Increasing technology, when accompanied by exploitative economic practices, and corporate efficiencies reduce the need for human workers and keep pay for essential employees low, thus perpetuating impoverishes many persons and makes poverty self-perpetuating. Therefore, we do not hold poor people morally responsible for their economic state. To begin to alleviate poverty, w  We support such policies as: adequate income maintenance, quality education, decent housing, job training, meaningful employment opportunities, adequate medical and hospital care, and humanization and radical revisions of welfare programs. But on another level, our society enjoys the benefits of low prices and of having dirty, monotonous, and often hazardous work done at low cost.  All employers, including churches and their agencies, have both a moral obligation and a civic responsibility to pay the true costs of good and services, too much of it now borne by the employed poor.  Justice requires the humanization and radical revision of the ways we think and act on the societal crime of employee poverty.   Since low wages are often a cause of poverty, employers should pay their employees a wage that does not require them to depend upon government subsidies such as food stamps or welfare for their livelihood.

Submitted ByJ. Gordon Chamberlin
Greensboro NC
