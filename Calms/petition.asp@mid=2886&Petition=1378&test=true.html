Submitted Petition Text:
General Conference shall establish a denominational health program, implemented by January 1, 2008, to be administered by The General Board of Pension and Health Benefits of The United Methodist Church (“General Board”) providing a benefit to all eligible active and retired clergy with design options at the conference level. This program should adhere to the critical success factors, the plan coverage and funding requirements included in the response document provided by the General Board attached hereto as Exhibit 1.
  

Exhibit 1
Response Document
General Board of Pension and Health Benefits of The United Methodist Church


Response to Annual Conferences’ Interest in a Denominational Health Plan

  

November 22, 2003
  
Definition of a Denominational Health Care Program

A denominational health care program would apply to all full-time active and retired United Methodist clergy.  Benefits would be provided through the programs administered by the General Board of Pension and Health Benefits (General Board).  Lay employees likely would not be required to participate, but, subject to their conference’s policy, would be allowed to participate.
  
All clergy would be assured of a minimum standard benefit plan (Appendix 1), but each conference would be able to provide optional benefit levels as their needs demand and their budgets allow.
  
Individual clergy would be allowed to opt out of the program; however, funding for all eligible clergy positions would still be required to avoid the financial consequences of adverse selection.  The position must be funded; participation would be optional.  
  
The General Conference would maintain authority over the program with the General Board acting at its behest  as administrator of the plan and its policies.
  

  
  
Factors to Consider

As of November 1, 2003, 24 of the 65 conferences and four United Methodist Church-affiliated organizations participate in the General Board's HealthFlex program.  HealthFlex provides benefits to 10,000 clergy and 3,000 lay employees plus their dependents.  By contrast, over 44,000 active and retired clergy participate in the General Board's retirement plans.  A denominational health plan would use the total clergy population to reduce administrative cost and offer greater network discounts.
  
According to a church-wide survey of conferences, from 2000 to 2001, 21 of the non-HealthFlex conferences that responded to the survey averaged, per employee per year, medical cost increases of approximately 30%.  Medical claims for HealthFlex conferences increased on average by approximately 15% over this same time period.  For retirees age 65+, the medical costs per retiree for non-HealthFlex conferences increased on average by 17% from 2000 to 2001.  For this same period, the average retiree medical cost increase for HealthFlex conferences was 12%.  
  
While results can vary year to year, smaller plans are increasingly disadvantaged in terms of critical mass purchasing and volatility of results.  
  
Churches are particularly disadvantaged in the funding of health care due to their small size and poor morbidity profile.  By way of comparison, the average increase in medical costs for all plan sponsors in the U.S. was approximately 11% (2000 to 2001).  
  
Many of the self-insured conferences advised the General Board that they are either in or approaching financial difficulty with respect to funding their health benefit programs.
  
Conference options are narrowing due to carrier consolidation, general health of the clergy population, and the limited market leverage as a relatively smaller group.  It is becoming increasingly difficult to get carriers to quote rates for insurance premiums, and, if quoted, second year rates are generally very high. If conditions continue, many conferences will face the difficult decision of dropping benefits, reducing staff or both.  

Position of the General Board of Pension and Health Benefits  

Recently, several annual conferences have approved petitions calling on the 2004 General Conference to establish a denominational health plan to be administered by the General Board. The General Board has developed the following statement to communicate its position on these annual conference initiatives.  
  
The General Board of Pension and Health Benefits (the General Board) has no  plans to sponsor the establishment of a denomination health program at the 2004 General Conference. However, as an observer and enabler of the health benefit needs of the denomination, the General Board recognizes the benefits that would be occasioned by such a plan. Such benefits include protected access to health benefits for clergy and lay employees, economies of scale in administration, and lower risk of financial catastrophe due to health care expenses for all participants.  
  
In order to ultimately support a mandatory denominational health benefit program, the General Board would consider the following criteria as paramount:  
  
The program authorization must:
  
  •    Provide for the necessary capital funding to establish such a program.
    • Establish a minimum core benefit for active and retired clergy.
      
  •    Provide for the establishment of reasonable competitive options and flexibility for plan sponsors, including vendor and plan design options beyond the core benefit.
    • Establish administration policy requiring consistent utilization of best underwriting practices regarding funding, eligibility, plan design, vendor selection and risk management.

    • Incorporate wellness and health as wholeness in the benefits design and administration policy.
        
    The General Board appreciates the work of the annual conferences to secure quality, lifelong health benefits for participants.

    Challenges
    The General Board recognizes that establishing a mandatory denominational health plan presents the following challenges.
      
      Transition
      
  • }}Virtually all conferences would see some change in their programs as they are moved to a uniform basis.  Benefit levels would vary depending on the conference plan.  However, design elections and administrative provisions (eligibility, covered medical services, termination, etc.) would be the same for all participants.  
     
  • }}
      Under-funded conferences
      
  • }}Conferences that are currently self-insured would have a liability for claims incurred prior to transition but not processed and paid as of that date.  If this liability has not been funded, a method of transitioning this liability would have to be worked out that does not unduly jeopardize the financial well being of the denominational plan, without imposing an unreasonable hardship on the conference.

      Communications
      
  • }}If a denominational plan is designed and other process and system enhancement-related changes are made, it would be necessary to be in frequent communication with participants, conference benefit officers and other program sponsors.

      Regional issues
      
  • }}Health care culture, delivery and opportunities vary by region within the U.S.  It would be important to implement a system for tracking and responding to these regional issues, as necessary, in addition to more global problems that affect all participants.

      Potential inadequacy of current systems
      
  • }}The various systems for tracking eligibility, enrollment, continuation coverage, and claims analysis must all be assessed as to the impact of (and opportunities presented by) a large influx of participants.

      Vendors coordination  
      
  • }}It is expected that it would be necessary to work with additional vendors for claims administration, data manipulation and analysis.  Coordination of these vendors would be complex, but necessary to avoid unnecessary duplication and expense.

      Rapid growth  
      
  • }}There are many problems that come with rapid growth of a benefit system.  Most are obvious and can be managed by adequate staff, either permanent or temporary.  A critical need would be the development of adequate and equitable premiums for all new conferences.  Premium from new conferences would flow in almost immediately; but the medical claims associated with these new participants come in more slowly, due to the nature of the claim adjudication process.  During the first year, premiums must be set correctly to cover all incurred claims and expenses.  Determining appropriate rate actions for the first renewal also would require careful analysis to assure the financial health of a denominational plan.

      •   Legal considerations
        Internal General Board counsel would need to review issues relating to plan design, documents and process changes.  Additional resources may be necessary due to the magnitude of the program.  Internal counsel also may need to consult with outside counsel.  

      Critical Success Factors        
        
      In order for any Denominational Health Care Plan to be successful, there are key principles that would have to be implemented:  
        
        All full-time active and retired clergy must be provided a core level of benefits.
        
    • }}
        The core level of benefits must provide for catastrophic financial protection and promote good health.  
        
    • }}
        Continuation of preferred risk pools, based on eligibility, will be essential and consideration should be given to at least 50% conference participation in the Health Risk Assessment (HRA) as a factor for continuation in the preferred risk pool.
        
    • }}
        Sufficient capital funding must be anticipated.  Mercer Human Resources Consulting has estimated that in addition to IBNR (incurred but not reported claims) reserves (developed through proper rate development), a denominational health plan should strive to develop free surplus of approximately 10% of annual claims.  This free surplus level is approximately equivalent to 50% of the projected IBNR reserve level.  Currently, IBNR reserves are approximately 20% of annual claims.  This free surplus is currently not present within most conferences but would provide better financial security for the entire denomination.  The actual dollar amount would be dependent upon the timing of the denominational plan enrollment.  However, the 10% of annual claims target could reasonably be achieved over a three-year period.  
        
    • }}
        Adequate rates must be set each year to assure that current year incurred expenses are funded (needed IBNR reserve increases funded by premiums).  
        
    • }}
        Accumulated free surpluses are maintained solely for the purpose of covering unforeseen deficits.  If surpluses exceed the target free surplus amount, the excess amount may, based on General Conference policy, be used to fund programs expected to positively impact the denomination’s health care costs.
        
    • }}
        Fair and equitable underwriting rules must be established that are administered uniformly, including rating methodology and pooling structure.
        
    •    A collaborative study should be commissioned that includes church leadership, general agencies and appropriate affiliated groups to explore and make recommendations at General Conference 2008 for ways to improve the long-term health of our clergy.  If a Denominational Health Plan is not implemented this quadrennium, the study also should provide to the 2008 General Conference a complete timeline and systematic implementation schedule for denominational health.
        
      Flexible Provisions

      A variety of conference options need to be established to allow conferences to customize their healthcare benefit program according to local standards and historical commitments.  Included in these options would be:
        
        Lay employee participation (Note: if offered on a voluntary basis, a different rate and underwriting structure may apply).
        
    • }}
        A reasonable range of higher benefit levels beyond the core requirement.  These optional plan designs would vary in terms of deductibles, co-pays, and out-of-pocket limits to provide program sponsors flexibility to be competitive within their geographical areas.  
        
    • }}
        Several disease and health management programs designed to address different problems that may be prevalent in some conferences more than others.
        
    • }}
        Flexible spending accounts to help participants budget and gain tax advantages.
        
    • }}
        The level of credibility assigned to the claims experience of conferences and the rules regarding 100% self-insurance (i.e., the pooling level) would be subject to demonstrated capital requirements and common actuarial principles.
        
    • }}
        Local HMO options, if financially feasible and contracted for by the General Board.  (Note:  would likely impact premiums charged for non-HMO plans offered).
        
      Capital Funding Requirements

      On an ongoing basis, we expect that a denominational program would operate at a lower administrative cost, as a percentage of claims, than the current HealthFlex program.  New streamlined processes and systems enhancements would also lower costs.  
        
      However, the changes being considered would involve start-up activities and related costs:
        
        Funding
        
    • }}As discussed in Critical Success Factors, sufficient capital funding must be anticipated.  The recommended level of funding has been established by Mercer.  The pace and method of funding will require the approval of the denomination.
       
    • }}
        Process Review and Documentation
        Additional staff or consultant budget would be needed to provide process review and documentation work.  Ongoing printing costs would be reduced by making greater use of the General Board’s website to publish standard letters, manuals and forms.
        
        Systems
        The General Board's health administration system would handle the additional eligibility requirements. The General Board’s systems vendor(s) should be asked to test the system and make any needed revisions or desirable enhancements.  Programming changes to PARK (the Participant record-keeping system) might require additional staffing or consulting fees.  
        
        Staffing
          Additional staff would need to be hired well in advance of the enrollment of new conferences to plan and execute changes to plan designs, processes, documentation, systems and other changes.  Capital expenditures would be required for recruitment and training, office equipment and supplies.  If the need is not expected to be ongoing, consultants or temporary contract employees may be substituted.  
        
        Vendor Selection
          A review of networks currently available under both the HealthFlex program and non-HealthFlex plans would be undertaken to determine where vendor changes would be desirable and begin the vendor selection process.  This may involve expenses for consulting services and travel for site visits and training.
        
        Rate Setting
          In order to finalize plan design options, there would be a need to calculate rates for several scenarios, perhaps considering several vendor arrangements.  This would require expenses for actuarial and consulting services.

        Legal
          Additional resources may be necessary due to the magnitude of the program.  Internal counsel may also need to consult with outside counsel.  

              Communications
          It would be necessary to be in frequent communication with participants, conference benefit officers and other program sponsors.  This would entail additional printing, production, handling and postage charges.
        
      Process and Timetable

      If the 2004 General Conference approves a denominational health plan, the plan could begin enrollment as early as January 1st, 2006 for some conferences and be in place in its entirety by January 1, 2008.  A phased-in approach would be recommended, putting the new plan design and vendor options in place and then bringing in the conferences on a voluntary basis prior to the January 1, 2008 mandatory date.  The study to improve clergy health would initially be convened in 2004.  Detailed implementation plans and schedules will require further study.

      RATIONALE FOR PETITION

          
    •    The Social Principles of The United Methodist Church recognize that health is inclusive of mental, social and spiritual well being and is both a public and private responsibility.  It is not just the absence of physical disease, which is the prevailing framework for the United States medical model.
          
          
    •    The Social Principles also recognize that access to adequate health care is a basic right.
          
          
    •    The health care industry in the United States is experiencing extreme cost increases, which place clergy and lay employees at risk of losing adequate health care coverage at an affordable cost.
          
          
    •    The loss of health care coverage can result in catastrophic financial loss for active and retired clergy and lay employees.
          
          
    •    Independent studies and surveys sponsored by the General Board have indicated that clergy health is at risk and has deteriorated over the last twenty years. In addition, this situation has dramatically increased the cost of health care to the denomination.
          
          
    •    Independent studies sponsored by the General Board indicate that a denominational health care plan could reduce costs by creating a greater pool to spread risk, increase purchasing power, reduce denominational health care trends and create a foundation to identify and help resolve broader issues of clergy health which can adversely impact the mission of the church.
          
          
    •    Pursuant to paragraph 1501a of the Book of Discipline, the General Board is responsible for the general supervision and administration of the support, relief, and assistance and pensioning of clergy, lay workers, and their families, of The United Methodist Church.
